What Is a Slip and Fall Claim?

A slip and fall claim is a type of premises liability case in which a person is injured because a property owner or occupier failed to maintain safe conditions. These accidents are among the most common causes of serious injury in California, resulting in broken bones, traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, and chronic pain that can last months or years.

If you were injured in a slip and fall accident in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in the Bay Area, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can evaluate your case and fight to recover the compensation you deserve. Property owners have a legal obligation to keep their premises reasonably safe, and when they fail to do so, they can be held accountable.

What the Law Says

Civil Code 1714 — General Duty of Care

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person." — California Civil Code Section 1714(a)

This is the foundational statute for premises liability in California. Civil Code 1714 establishes that every property owner and occupier owes a duty of ordinary care to anyone who comes onto their property. This means they must inspect for hazards, repair dangerous conditions within a reasonable time, and warn visitors of risks that are not obvious. The duty applies to homeowners, landlords, business owners, and government entities alike.

Under the landmark case Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, California courts evaluate whether a property owner breached this duty by considering factors including the foreseeability of harm, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury, and the policy of preventing future harm.

Civil Code 846 — Recreational Use Immunity

"An owner of any estate or any other interest in real property, whether possessory or nonpossessory, owes no duty of care to keep the premises safe for entry or use by others for any recreational purpose or to give any warning of hazardous conditions... where no consideration is given." — California Civil Code Section 846

Civil Code 846 provides a limited immunity for property owners when a person enters their land for recreational purposes without paying a fee. This statute is often raised as a defense in cases involving injuries on rural land, trails, or private open spaces. However, the immunity does not apply when the owner charges for access, when the injury is caused by the owner's willful or malicious failure to guard against a dangerous condition, or when the injured person was expressly invited onto the property.

Government Code 835 — Dangerous Condition of Public Property

"A public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and that either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition, or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition." — California Government Code Section 835

When a slip and fall occurs on government-owned property — such as a public sidewalk in Santa Clara County, a city park in San Jose, or a government building in San Francisco — the injured person must prove that the public entity had notice of the dangerous condition and failed to correct it. Importantly, claims against government entities must be filed within six months under Government Code 911.2, a much shorter window than the standard two-year statute of limitations.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how premises liability claims commonly arise in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Wet Grocery Store Floor

A shopper at a grocery store in San Jose slips on a puddle of water near the produce section. The spill had been on the floor for over 30 minutes, and no wet floor sign was posted. The store had a policy requiring employees to check the floors every 15 minutes but no inspection was performed. The shopper suffers a broken hip and requires surgery. Because the store had constructive notice of the hazard and failed to clean it up or warn customers, the store may be liable under Civil Code 1714.

Example 2 — Broken Stairwell in an Apartment Building

A tenant in a San Francisco apartment building trips on a cracked step in a poorly lit stairwell and falls down a flight of stairs, sustaining a traumatic brain injury. Multiple tenants had submitted maintenance requests about the broken step over the previous three months, but the landlord never made repairs. California landlords have a duty under Civil Code 1941 to maintain rental units in habitable condition, and the repeated complaints establish actual notice of the dangerous condition.

Example 3 — Parking Lot Pothole

A woman walking to her car in a shopping center parking lot in Santa Clara steps into a large pothole that had been left unrepaired for months and twists her ankle severely, tearing ligaments. The property management company responsible for maintaining the lot had received complaints from multiple business tenants about the deteriorating pavement. The failure to repair a known hazard in a high-traffic area is strong evidence of negligence.

Example 4 — Restaurant Spill

A diner at a restaurant in Gilroy slips on grease that had spilled from the kitchen into the dining area. The restaurant staff was aware of the recurring grease issue but had not placed mats or taken steps to prevent the spill from reaching the walkway. The patron suffers a fractured wrist and torn rotator cuff. The restaurant's failure to address a recurring hazard that it knew about demonstrates a breach of the duty of care owed to business invitees.

California's pure comparative fault rule means your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault but never eliminated entirely. If a jury finds that you were 20% at fault for not watching where you were walking, and your total damages are $500,000, you would still recover $400,000. This makes it critical to build a strong case demonstrating the property owner's greater share of responsibility.

How We Help

Premises liability cases require thorough investigation and strategic advocacy. Here is how our attorneys build winning slip and fall cases for clients throughout the Bay Area:

1. Proving Notice of the Hazard

The most critical element in any slip and fall case is proving that the property owner knew — or should have known — about the dangerous condition. We gather surveillance footage, maintenance logs, employee schedules, prior incident reports, and witness statements to establish that the owner had actual or constructive notice of the hazard. In cases involving recurring problems, we document the pattern of negligence that shows the owner's awareness over time. Time is essential, because evidence like security camera footage is often overwritten within days or weeks.

2. Overcoming Comparative Fault Arguments

Property owners and their insurers almost always argue that the injured person was partially or entirely at fault — claiming they were not paying attention, wearing inappropriate footwear, or should have seen the hazard. We counter these arguments with evidence showing that the hazard was not open and obvious, that the property owner's negligence created the primary cause of the accident, and that a reasonable person in the same circumstances would not have anticipated the danger. We also work with accident reconstruction experts when needed to demonstrate exactly how the fall occurred.

3. Government Claims Process

When a slip and fall occurs on government-owned property — a San Jose sidewalk, a Santa Clara County park, or a state building — the injured person must file a formal claim with the appropriate government entity within six months of the incident under Government Code 911.2. Failure to meet this deadline can permanently bar the claim. We handle the entire government claims process, including identifying the correct entity, preparing the claim, and filing within the deadline. If the claim is denied, we file suit within six months of the rejection under Government Code 945.6.

4. Expert Witnesses

Complex premises liability cases often require expert testimony to prove negligence and causation. We work with safety engineers who can identify building code violations, biomechanical experts who can explain how the fall caused the specific injuries, medical professionals who can project the cost of future treatment, and economists who can calculate lost earning capacity. Expert testimony can transform a disputed case into a compelling narrative of clear property owner negligence.

5. Documenting Injuries and Damages

Insurance companies routinely undervalue slip and fall claims by minimizing the severity of injuries or arguing that the injuries were pre-existing. We work closely with your medical team to obtain detailed records documenting the full extent of your injuries, create a comprehensive accounting of all medical expenses (past and projected future costs), document the impact on your daily life and work through personal declarations and vocational assessments, and build a damages model that reflects the true cost of your injury — not the lowball figure the insurer wants to pay.

6. Litigation and Trial

Most premises liability cases settle before trial, but insurance companies are more likely to offer fair settlements when they know your attorney is prepared to go to court. Our trial-ready approach means we prepare every case as if it will be heard by a jury — conducting thorough discovery, deposing key witnesses, retaining qualified experts, and filing aggressive motions. When the insurance company refuses to offer a reasonable settlement, we take the case to trial in Santa Clara County Superior Court, San Francisco Superior Court, or wherever your case is filed.

To prevail in a California premises liability case, you must prove four elements: (1) the defendant owned, leased, occupied, or controlled the property; (2) the defendant was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; (3) you were harmed; and (4) the defendant's negligence was a substantial factor in causing your harm. A key part of element (2) is proving that the property owner knew or should have known about the dangerous condition — referred to as actual or constructive notice.

The property owner must have had either actual notice (they knew about the hazard) or constructive notice (the hazard existed long enough that a reasonable owner would have discovered it through ordinary inspection). For example, if a spill in a store had been on the floor for 45 minutes, a court could find that the store should have discovered it through routine inspections. The longer the hazard existed, the stronger the argument for constructive notice.

After a slip and fall, take these steps if you are able: (1) report the incident to the property owner or manager and request a written incident report; (2) photograph the hazardous condition, your injuries, and the surrounding area; (3) get the names and contact information of any witnesses; (4) seek medical attention promptly, even if your injuries seem minor at first; (5) preserve the clothing and footwear you were wearing; and (6) contact a premises liability attorney before speaking with the property owner's insurance company. Early evidence preservation is often the difference between a strong case and a weak one.
