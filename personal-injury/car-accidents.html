Car Accidents in California

California leads the nation in traffic-related injuries and fatalities, and the Bay Area is no exception. From congested freeway corridors like Highway 101 and Interstate 280 to busy city intersections in San Jose, San Francisco, and Santa Clara County, car accidents happen every day. When they do, the consequences can be devastating — broken bones, traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, and in the worst cases, wrongful death.

California Law

Civil Code 1714 — General Negligence

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person..." — California Civil Code Section 1714(a)

Civil Code 1714 is the foundation of nearly every car accident claim in California. It establishes that all people have a duty to exercise ordinary care to avoid injuring others. When a driver fails to exercise that care — by speeding, running a red light, texting behind the wheel, or driving under the influence — and that failure causes an accident, the driver is negligent and liable for the resulting injuries and damages.

Vehicle Code 17150 — Owner Liability

"Every owner of a motor vehicle is liable and responsible for death or injury to person or property resulting from a negligent or wrongful act or omission in the operation of the motor vehicle... by any person using or operating the same with the permission, express or implied, of the owner." — California Vehicle Code Section 17150

Under CVC 17150, the owner of a vehicle can be held liable for injuries caused by anyone driving the car with the owner's permission. This is significant in cases involving borrowed vehicles, company cars, or family members driving someone else's vehicle. If the at-fault driver was operating a vehicle with the owner's consent, both the driver and the owner may be responsible for your damages.

Vehicle Code 20001 — Hit and Run Causing Injury

"The driver of a vehicle involved in an accident resulting in injury to a person, other than himself or herself... shall immediately stop the vehicle at the scene of the accident..." — California Vehicle Code Section 20001(a)

California law requires every driver involved in an accident causing injury to stop at the scene, provide identification, and render reasonable assistance. Fleeing the scene of an injury accident is a felony under CVC 20001, punishable by up to three years in state prison. Hit-and-run accidents are unfortunately common throughout San Jose and the Bay Area, but victims still have legal options for recovering compensation through uninsured motorist coverage and civil claims.

Comparative Fault — Civil Code 1714 & Li v. Yellow Cab Co.

California follows a pure comparative negligence system, established by the California Supreme Court in Li v. Yellow Cab Co. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 804. A plaintiff's recovery is reduced by the percentage of fault attributable to the plaintiff, but is not barred entirely regardless of the plaintiff's degree of fault. — Li v. Yellow Cab Co. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 804

Under California's pure comparative fault system, you can recover damages even if you were partially responsible for the accident. Your total recovery is simply reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if a jury finds you were 30% at fault and your total damages are $200,000, you would recover $140,000. This rule is particularly important in intersection accidents and lane-change collisions where both drivers may share some degree of responsibility.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios reflect the types of car accident cases our attorneys handle throughout the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Rear-End Collision on Highway 101

A commuter traveling southbound on Highway 101 near the De La Cruz exit in Santa Clara is struck from behind by a distracted driver who was looking at a cell phone. The impact causes the victim to suffer whiplash, a herniated disc, and a concussion. Even though the injuries did not require surgery, the victim misses eight weeks of work and incurs over $45,000 in medical bills. The at-fault driver's insurer initially offers $12,000. After filing a lawsuit, the case settles for a significantly higher amount reflecting the true cost of the injuries.

Example 2 — Intersection T-Bone in San Jose

A driver runs a red light at the intersection of Story Road and King Road in San Jose, striking another vehicle broadside on the driver's side door. The victim suffers a fractured pelvis, broken ribs, and internal bleeding, requiring emergency surgery and weeks of hospitalization. The at-fault driver's insurance company disputes fault, claiming the light was yellow. Surveillance camera footage from a nearby business confirms the light was red, and the victim's attorney uses this evidence to establish clear liability.

Example 3 — Hit-and-Run in San Francisco

A pedestrian crossing at a marked crosswalk on Mission Street in San Francisco is struck by a vehicle that immediately flees the scene. The victim suffers a traumatic brain injury and multiple fractures. Although the at-fault driver is never identified, the victim's own uninsured motorist (UM) coverage provides a path to compensation. An experienced attorney helps the victim file a UM claim and negotiates a fair settlement with the victim's own insurance carrier.

Example 4 — Rideshare Accident in the Bay Area

A passenger in an Uber traveling through downtown San Jose is injured when the rideshare driver runs a stop sign and collides with a delivery truck. The passenger suffers a broken wrist and soft tissue injuries to the neck and back. Rideshare accident claims involve multiple layers of insurance coverage — the driver's personal policy, the rideshare company's commercial policy, and the other driver's coverage. An attorney determines which policies apply and pursues the maximum recovery from all available sources.

What's at Stake

The damages available in a California car accident case depend on the severity of your injuries, the impact on your daily life, and the conduct of the at-fault driver. Here are the main categories of compensation:

Type of Damages Category Description
Medical Expenses Economic Hospital bills, surgery, physical therapy, medications, future medical care, and rehabilitation costs
Lost Wages Economic Income lost during recovery, reduced earning capacity, lost employment benefits, and future lost earnings
Pain & Suffering Non-Economic Physical pain, emotional distress, anxiety, depression, loss of enjoyment of life, and loss of consortium
Property Damage Economic Vehicle repair or replacement, personal property destroyed in the crash, rental car expenses
Punitive Damages Exemplary Awarded in rare cases involving egregious conduct such as drunk driving or intentional recklessness (Civil Code 3294)

Important note: California does not cap non-economic damages in personal injury cases (unlike medical malpractice cases, which are capped under MICRA). This means there is no statutory limit on the amount you can recover for pain and suffering in a car accident claim. The value of your case depends on the specific facts — the severity of injury, the length of treatment, the impact on your ability to work and enjoy life, and the strength of the evidence supporting your claim.

How We Help

Every car accident case is different. Our attorneys develop a tailored strategy based on the facts of your case to maximize your recovery. Here is how we approach car accident claims for clients throughout San Jose, San Francisco, and the Bay Area:

1. Proving Negligence

The foundation of any car accident claim is proving that the other driver was negligent. We gather and preserve critical evidence — police reports, witness statements, traffic camera footage, cell phone records, and accident reconstruction analysis — to establish that the at-fault driver breached their duty of care. In cases involving disputed liability, we retain expert witnesses who can reconstruct the accident and testify about the cause and mechanics of the collision.

2. Dealing with Insurance Companies

Insurance companies are in the business of minimizing payouts. Their adjusters are trained to take recorded statements, pressure you into quick settlements, and use your own words against you. We handle all communications with the insurance company on your behalf, ensuring that nothing you say is used to undermine your claim. We know the tactics insurers use, and we counter them with thorough documentation and aggressive negotiation.

3. Comparative Fault Arguments

The opposing insurer will often try to shift blame onto you to reduce the amount they owe. Under California's comparative fault system, even a small percentage of fault attributed to you can significantly reduce your recovery. We fight back against these arguments by presenting evidence that clearly establishes the other driver's responsibility. When our clients bear some degree of fault, we work to minimize that percentage through strategic presentation of the evidence.

4. Uninsured & Underinsured Motorist Claims

Approximately 15% of California drivers are uninsured. If you are hit by an uninsured driver — or a driver whose policy limits are insufficient to cover your damages — your own uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage may provide compensation. These claims are filed against your own insurance carrier, which can be adversarial despite the fact that you are their customer. We handle UM/UIM claims with the same rigor as third-party claims, including arbitration when necessary.

5. Maximizing Your Damages

Many accident victims underestimate the true cost of their injuries. We work with medical professionals, economists, and life-care planners to calculate the full extent of your damages — including future medical treatment, long-term loss of earning capacity, and the ongoing impact of chronic pain or disability on your quality of life. We do not settle for less than what your case is worth, and we document every element of your damages to support a maximum recovery.

6. Litigation & Trial

Most car accident cases settle before trial, but some do not. When the insurance company refuses to offer fair compensation, we are prepared to take your case to court. Our attorneys have trial experience in Santa Clara County Superior Court and courts throughout the Bay Area. The willingness and ability to go to trial gives us leverage in settlement negotiations — insurance companies know that we will not accept a lowball offer simply to avoid litigation.
