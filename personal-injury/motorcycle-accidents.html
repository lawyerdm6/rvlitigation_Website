Motorcycle Accidents in California

California has more registered motorcycles than any other state, and the Bay Area's year-round riding conditions make motorcyclists a constant presence on highways and surface streets from San Jose to San Francisco. But that prevalence comes with disproportionate risk. According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, motorcyclists are 29 times more likely to die in a traffic collision than occupants of passenger vehicles per mile traveled.

When a car, truck, or SUV collides with a motorcycle, the rider absorbs nearly the full force of impact. The injuries are often catastrophic — traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, road rash requiring skin grafts, and crushed or amputated limbs. If you or a loved one has been hurt in a motorcycle accident in Santa Clara County, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in the Bay Area, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can help you pursue the compensation you deserve.

What the Law Says

Vehicle Code 21658.1 — Lane Splitting

"The Department of the California Highway Patrol may develop educational guidelines relating to lane splitting in a manner that would ensure the safety of the motorcyclist and the drivers and passengers of the surrounding vehicles." — California Vehicle Code Section 21658.1 (AB 51, effective 2017)

California is the only state in the nation that explicitly authorizes lane splitting — the practice of a motorcyclist riding between lanes of slow or stopped traffic. While the statute itself does not set specific speed limits for lane splitting, the CHP's guidelines recommend doing so only when traffic is moving at 30 mph or less and the motorcyclist is traveling no more than 10 mph faster than surrounding traffic. When a driver changes lanes without checking mirrors or opens a car door into a motorcyclist's path, that driver — not the rider — is typically at fault.

Vehicle Code 27803 — Helmet Requirements

"A driver and any passenger shall wear a safety helmet meeting requirements established by the Department of Transportation when riding on a motorcycle, motor-driven cycle, or motorized bicycle." — California Vehicle Code Section 27803(a)

California mandates DOT-approved helmets for all motorcycle riders and passengers, regardless of age or experience. Failure to wear a helmet does not prevent you from filing a personal injury claim, but it can be raised as a comparative negligence defense to reduce your recovery — particularly if you suffered a head injury. Insurance companies routinely exploit helmet non-compliance to minimize payouts, which is why experienced legal representation matters.

Civil Code 1714 — General Duty of Care

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person..." — California Civil Code Section 1714(a)

This foundational statute establishes the legal duty that every driver on California roads owes to every other person, including motorcyclists. A driver who fails to exercise ordinary care — by running a red light, failing to yield, texting while driving, or making an unsafe lane change — breaches this duty. When that breach causes a motorcycle collision, the injured rider has the right to recover damages for all resulting harm.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios reflect the types of motorcycle accident cases we see in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Left-Turn Collision

A motorcyclist traveling northbound on Winchester Boulevard in San Jose has a green light and is proceeding through an intersection. An oncoming SUV attempts a left turn and strikes the rider. The SUV driver failed to yield the right of way under CVC 21801. The motorcyclist suffers a fractured femur, torn rotator cuff, and road rash. The driver is liable for the rider's medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

Example 2 — Lane-Splitting Crash on Highway 101

During rush-hour traffic on US-101 near San Francisco, a motorcyclist is lawfully lane splitting at approximately 25 mph between stopped vehicles. A distracted driver in the left lane suddenly changes lanes without signaling, clipping the rider and sending them into the center divider. Because the motorcyclist was operating within CHP lane-splitting guidelines, the driver bears primary fault for the collision.

Example 3 — Dooring on a City Street

A motorcycle rider in downtown San Jose is traveling in the right lane when a parked car's driver opens their door directly into the rider's path. The motorcyclist strikes the door, is thrown from the bike, and suffers a concussion, broken collarbone, and severe road rash. Under CVC 22517, no person shall open a vehicle door on the side of moving traffic unless it is safe to do so. The car's occupant is liable.

Example 4 — Hit-and-Run in Gilroy

A motorcyclist riding on Monterey Road in Gilroy is rear-ended by a pickup truck that immediately flees the scene. The rider sustains spinal injuries requiring surgery. Even though the at-fault driver left, the motorcyclist may still recover compensation through their own uninsured motorist (UM) coverage. An attorney can also work with law enforcement to identify the hit-and-run driver and pursue a direct claim.

What's at Stake

Motorcycle accident injuries are among the most severe of any traffic collision. The damages available depend on the nature of your injuries, the impact on your daily life, and whether the at-fault party's conduct was particularly reckless.

Damage Category Type Description
Medical Expenses Economic Emergency care, surgery, hospitalization, rehabilitation, future treatment
Lost Wages Economic Past income lost and future earning capacity diminished by the injury
Property Damage Economic Motorcycle repair or replacement, gear, equipment
Pain & Suffering Non-Economic Physical pain, emotional distress, anxiety, PTSD, loss of enjoyment of life
Loss of Consortium Non-Economic Impact on marital or family relationships due to the injury
Punitive Damages Punitive Awarded in cases of drunk driving, extreme recklessness, or intentional misconduct

California's comparative fault rule: Under California's pure comparative negligence system (Li v. Yellow Cab Co., 1975), you can recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. Your award is reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found 20% at fault and your damages total $500,000, you would recover $400,000. Insurance companies aggressively argue comparative fault to reduce payouts — particularly in motorcycle cases where bias against riders is common.

How We Help

Motorcycle accident claims require aggressive, detail-oriented representation. Insurance companies routinely undervalue motorcycle injury claims and exploit anti-rider bias. Here is how RV Litigation Group PC fights for our clients throughout the Bay Area:

1. Thorough Accident Investigation

We move quickly to preserve critical evidence before it disappears. Our team obtains police reports, interviews witnesses, secures surveillance and dashcam footage, photographs the scene, and works with accident reconstruction experts when necessary. In motorcycle cases, physical evidence such as skid marks, gouge patterns, and debris fields can prove exactly how the collision occurred and who was at fault.

2. Defeating the Anti-Rider Bias

Insurance adjusters — and sometimes juries — harbor an unconscious bias against motorcyclists, assuming the rider must have been speeding or riding recklessly. We proactively counter this prejudice by documenting the rider's experience, safety training, helmet use, proper licensing, and adherence to traffic laws. We reframe the narrative so the focus stays where it belongs: on the negligent driver who caused the crash.

3. Maximizing Your Medical Recovery Documentation

The value of your claim is directly tied to the quality of your medical documentation. We work with your treating physicians to ensure every injury, every procedure, and every limitation is thoroughly documented. We retain medical experts who can explain the long-term consequences of traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, or orthopedic injuries to an insurance company or jury in clear, compelling terms.

4. Aggressive Insurance Negotiations

We do not accept lowball settlement offers. Our attorneys understand the full scope of damages available under California law — including future medical costs, diminished earning capacity, and pain and suffering — and we demand full compensation. When the insurer refuses to pay fair value, we are prepared to take the case to trial. Insurance companies settle for more when they know your attorney is willing to litigate.

5. Identifying All Liable Parties

Motorcycle accidents often involve multiple sources of liability. Beyond the negligent driver, we investigate whether a defective motorcycle component contributed to the crash (product liability), whether a government entity failed to maintain safe road conditions (dangerous road design, potholes, inadequate signage), or whether an employer is vicariously liable for a driver who was on the job. More defendants mean more insurance coverage available to compensate you.

6. Wrongful Death and Catastrophic Injury Claims

When a motorcycle accident results in death or permanently disabling injuries, the stakes are enormous. We handle wrongful death claims on behalf of surviving family members under CCP 377.60, pursuing compensation for funeral expenses, loss of financial support, and loss of companionship. For catastrophic injuries such as paralysis or amputation, we retain life-care planners to project the full cost of future care so that your settlement or verdict covers your needs for a lifetime.
