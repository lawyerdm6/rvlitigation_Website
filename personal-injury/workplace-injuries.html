What Are Workplace Injury Claims?

A workplace injury claim arises when a worker is hurt on the job due to unsafe conditions, defective equipment, employer negligence, or the carelessness of a third party. California's workers' compensation system provides a no-fault insurance framework that covers medical treatment and disability benefits for injured employees — but it also limits the worker's ability to sue their own employer. What many injured workers do not realize is that when a third party contributed to the injury, a separate personal injury lawsuit can unlock significantly greater compensation, including pain and suffering, full lost earnings, and punitive damages.

If you were injured at work in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, Sunnyvale, or anywhere in Santa Clara County or the greater Bay Area, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can help you pursue every available avenue of recovery. We represent injured workers in third-party liability claims, Cal/OSHA violation cases, product liability actions, and construction site accident litigation throughout California.

California leads the nation in workplace safety regulation, yet thousands of workers are seriously injured every year in preventable accidents. In Santa Clara County alone, industries ranging from construction and manufacturing to technology and logistics expose workers to hazards including falls from height, heavy machinery, toxic chemicals, repetitive stress injuries, and motor vehicle collisions. Workers' compensation provides a safety net, but it was never designed to fully compensate a seriously injured person. Third-party claims fill that gap — allowing injured workers to hold the actual wrongdoers accountable and recover the full measure of their damages.

At RV Litigation Group PC, we take a dual-track approach to every workplace injury case. We ensure our clients receive the workers' compensation benefits they are entitled to while simultaneously investigating and pursuing third-party claims that can dramatically increase their total recovery. Our attorneys understand the interplay between these two systems and how to maximize results in both.

What the Law Says

Labor Code 3600 — Workers' Compensation Exclusivity

"Liability for the compensation provided by this division, in lieu of any other liability whatsoever to any person... shall, without regard to negligence, exist against an employer for any injury sustained by his or her employees arising out of and in the course of the employment..." — California Labor Code Section 3600(a)

Labor Code 3600 establishes the exclusive remedy doctrine — the cornerstone of California workers' compensation law. Under this provision, workers' compensation benefits are the sole remedy an injured employee has against their employer for a workplace injury. In exchange for guaranteed, no-fault benefits (medical treatment, temporary disability, permanent disability, and supplemental job displacement), the employee gives up the right to sue the employer in civil court. This trade-off means workers do not have to prove employer negligence, but it also means they cannot recover pain and suffering, emotional distress, or punitive damages through the workers' comp system.

There are narrow exceptions to this exclusivity rule. An employee may sue the employer directly if the employer committed an intentional assault, fraudulently concealed a workplace hazard, or removed or failed to install a power press safety guard (Labor Code 4558). Outside of these narrow exceptions, the workers' comp system is the exclusive path for claims against the employer.

Labor Code 3852 — Third-Party Liability Claims

"The claim of an employee... for compensation does not affect his or her claim or right of action for all damages proximately resulting from the injury or death against any person other than the employer." — California Labor Code Section 3852

This is the statute that opens the door to full compensation for injured workers. Labor Code 3852 expressly preserves the employee's right to bring a civil lawsuit against any third party — anyone other than the employer — whose negligence, recklessness, or defective product caused the workplace injury. Third-party defendants can include general contractors, subcontractors on multi-employer job sites, equipment manufacturers, property owners, maintenance companies, and negligent motorists.

Critically, a third-party personal injury claim operates under standard tort law principles, which means the injured worker can recover all compensatory damages: past and future medical expenses, lost wages and diminished earning capacity, pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and in cases of egregious conduct, punitive damages. The workers' compensation carrier has a right of reimbursement (a lien) against any third-party recovery under Labor Code 3856, but the net result is almost always a far greater recovery than workers' comp alone.

Cal/OSHA — Labor Code 6300 et seq.

"Every employer shall furnish employment and a place of employment that is safe and healthful for the employees therein... No employer shall require, or permit any employee to go or be in any employment or place of employment which is not safe and healthful." — California Labor Code Section 6400(a)–(b)

California's Occupational Safety and Health Act (Cal/OSHA) imposes comprehensive safety requirements on every employer in the state. Under Labor Code 6401.7, employers must establish, implement, and maintain a written Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP). Cal/OSHA sets specific standards for fall protection, scaffolding, hazardous materials handling, machine guarding, confined spaces, personal protective equipment, and hundreds of other workplace conditions. Violations are investigated by the Division of Occupational Safety and Health (DOSH), which can impose citations and penalties ranging from $18,000 per serious violation to $180,000 for willful or repeat violations.

While a Cal/OSHA citation alone does not create a private right of action, evidence of safety violations is highly relevant in third-party liability claims. A Cal/OSHA violation can establish a defendant's negligence per se — meaning the violation of a safety regulation is itself evidence of negligence — which strengthens the injured worker's case significantly. Our attorneys routinely obtain and analyze Cal/OSHA investigation files, citation histories, and inspection records to build the strongest possible claims for our clients.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how workplace injury and third-party liability claims commonly arise across the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Construction Fall in San Jose

A framing carpenter working on a residential construction project in San Jose falls 20 feet from a scaffold that lacked required guardrails and toeboards. He suffers a fractured pelvis, two broken vertebrae, and a traumatic brain injury. His employer's workers' compensation insurance covers his medical treatment and temporary disability payments, but the benefits are capped and do not compensate for his chronic pain or diminished quality of life. Investigation reveals that the general contractor controlled the job site and was responsible for providing fall protection under Cal/OSHA Title 8, Section 1635. The carpenter files a third-party liability claim against the general contractor under Labor Code 3852, seeking full compensatory damages for his catastrophic injuries — including pain and suffering, future medical care, and lost earning capacity as a tradesman.

Example 2 — Factory Equipment Malfunction in Fremont

A machine operator at a manufacturing plant in Fremont loses three fingers when an industrial press cycles unexpectedly during a maintenance procedure. The lockout/tagout procedure was not followed, and the press itself had a defective safety interlock switch that should have prevented the machine from activating when the guard was open. Workers' compensation covers his surgery and permanent disability rating, but the benefits do not reflect the full impact of losing the use of his dominant hand. The worker files a product liability claim against the press manufacturer for the defective safety interlock and a negligence claim against the maintenance contractor that serviced the machine six weeks before the accident and failed to identify the faulty switch. Cal/OSHA's investigation confirms multiple violations of Title 8, Section 4184 (mechanical power press safeguards).

Example 3 — Toxic Exposure at Tech Facility in Sunnyvale

A facilities maintenance worker at a semiconductor fabrication plant in Sunnyvale develops severe respiratory disease after years of exposure to hydrofluoric acid, arsine gas, and other toxic chemicals used in chip manufacturing. His employer provided inadequate ventilation and failed to ensure proper use of respiratory protection as required by Cal/OSHA's Airborne Contaminants standards (Title 8, Sections 5155 and 5144). Workers' compensation covers his ongoing medical treatment, but the occupational disease claim is complex and the benefits do not address his long-term suffering. The worker files a third-party claim against the chemical suppliers who failed to provide adequate safety data sheets and warnings, and against the ventilation system contractor whose negligent design created the hazardous exposure conditions.

Example 4 — Delivery Driver Hit by Third Party in Morgan Hill

A delivery driver making a route stop on Monterey Road in Morgan Hill is rear-ended by a distracted driver while parked in a loading zone with hazard lights activated. The collision causes herniated discs at C5-C6 and L4-L5, a torn rotator cuff, and post-traumatic stress disorder. Workers' compensation covers a portion of his medical treatment and temporary disability, but the no-fault system does not compensate him for pain and suffering, emotional trauma, or the full extent of his future lost wages. Because the injury was caused entirely by a negligent third-party motorist, the driver files a personal injury lawsuit under Labor Code 3852 against the at-fault driver, seeking the full range of tort damages. The workers' compensation carrier asserts a lien under Labor Code 3856, but the third-party recovery still far exceeds what workers' comp alone would have provided.

What's at Stake

The difference between a workers' compensation claim and a third-party liability claim can be enormous. Here is a comparison of what each system provides:

Damage Category Workers' Compensation Third-Party Claim
Medical Expenses All reasonable and necessary treatment (subject to utilization review) Full past and future medical costs without utilization review caps
Lost Wages Temporary disability: two-thirds of average weekly wage, capped (~$1,620/week in 2024) Full lost wages and future earning capacity — no cap
Permanent Disability Rated by AMA Guides; weekly payments based on disability percentage (max ~$350/week) Full compensation for permanent impairment and diminished quality of life
Pain & Suffering Not available Full recovery for physical pain, mental anguish, and loss of enjoyment of life
Emotional Distress Not available Recoverable as general and/or special damages
Punitive Damages Not available Available when defendant acted with malice, oppression, or fraud (CC 3294)
Death Benefits $250,000–$320,000 to dependents (LC 4702); burial up to $10,000 Full wrongful death damages: loss of support, companionship, guidance — no cap

Key takeaway: Workers' compensation provides guaranteed, immediate benefits regardless of fault — but the system was designed to limit employer liability, not to fully compensate the injured worker. A third-party claim restores the worker's right to full tort damages, including the categories that workers' comp excludes entirely: pain and suffering, emotional distress, and punitive damages. In catastrophic injury cases involving paralysis, traumatic brain injuries, amputations, or death, the difference between the two systems can be hundreds of thousands or even millions of dollars.

How We Help

At RV Litigation Group PC, we take a comprehensive, dual-track approach to every workplace injury case. Here is how we maximize recovery for injured workers:

1. Identifying Third-Party Defendants

The most critical step in a workplace injury case is identifying every potentially liable third party. Many injured workers — and even some attorneys — stop at workers' compensation without realizing that a third-party claim exists. We conduct thorough investigations to determine whether a general contractor, subcontractor, equipment manufacturer, property owner, maintenance company, architect or engineer, or negligent motorist contributed to the injury. In complex construction and industrial cases, there are often multiple third-party defendants, each carrying separate insurance policies. Identifying all of them is essential to maximizing the total recovery.

2. Recovering Beyond Workers' Comp

Workers' compensation benefits are capped by statute and do not include pain and suffering, emotional distress, or punitive damages. Our goal is to recover the full measure of your damages through third-party claims. We calculate your complete losses — including future medical care needs, lifetime earning capacity, and the non-economic impact of your injuries — and pursue claims that reflect the true cost of what happened to you. We also negotiate workers' comp lien reductions under Labor Code 3856 and 3860 to maximize your net recovery after accounting for the carrier's reimbursement rights.

3. Leveraging Cal/OSHA Violations

Cal/OSHA violations are powerful evidence in third-party liability cases. When a general contractor, property owner, or other responsible party violated specific safety regulations — such as fall protection standards, machine guarding requirements, hazardous materials handling rules, or confined space protocols — that violation can establish negligence per se under Evidence Code 669. We obtain Cal/OSHA investigation reports, citation records, and inspection histories to build the strongest possible case. In cases where Cal/OSHA has not yet investigated, we may file a complaint on behalf of the client to trigger an inspection that generates valuable evidence for the civil case.

4. Product Liability Claims

Many workplace injuries are caused or worsened by defective equipment, tools, or safety devices. Under California's strict product liability doctrine (Greenman v. Yuba Power Products, 59 Cal.2d 57 (1963)), a manufacturer is strictly liable for injuries caused by a defective product — regardless of whether the manufacturer was negligent. We investigate whether the equipment involved in your injury had design defects, manufacturing defects, or inadequate warnings. We work with engineering experts to analyze the product, review recall histories, and build claims against manufacturers, distributors, and suppliers. Product liability claims can result in substantial recoveries, particularly when the defect affected multiple workers.

5. Construction Site Liability

Construction sites in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, and throughout Santa Clara County are among the most dangerous workplaces in California. Under Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689 and its progeny, a hiring entity is generally not liable for injuries to a contractor's employees. However, critical exceptions apply when the hiring entity retained control over safety conditions (Hooker v. Department of Transportation, 2002), exercised affirmative control that contributed to the injury, or when a landowner knew of a concealed hazard and failed to warn (Kinsman v. Unocal Corp., 2005). We analyze the contractual relationships, site control dynamics, and safety responsibilities on every construction case to identify liable parties.

6. Trial

When insurance companies refuse to offer fair compensation, we are prepared to take your case to trial. Our attorneys have tried workplace injury and personal injury cases in Santa Clara County Superior Court, San Francisco Superior Court, Alameda County Superior Court, and federal courts throughout the Northern District of California. Workplace injury cases are compelling to juries — they involve real people who were just doing their jobs when someone else's negligence changed their lives. We present your story with the evidence, expert testimony, and courtroom skill needed to achieve justice.

Frequently Asked Questions

However, you can file a third-party liability lawsuit against any party other than your employer who contributed to your injury — such as a subcontractor, equipment manufacturer, property owner, or negligent driver. Third-party claims allow you to recover full compensatory damages including pain and suffering, which are not available through workers' comp.

A third-party claim is a personal injury lawsuit filed against someone other than your employer who caused or contributed to your workplace injury. Under Labor Code 3852, an injured worker may bring an action at law for damages against any third party whose negligence, defective product, or wrongful conduct caused the injury. Common third-party defendants include general contractors, subcontractors, equipment manufacturers, property owners, and other motorists in work-related vehicle accidents.

For workers' compensation claims, you generally have one year from the date of injury to file a claim with the Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB) under Labor Code 5405. For third-party personal injury claims, the statute of limitations is two years from the date of injury under Code of Civil Procedure 335.1. For Cal/OSHA complaints, there is a six-month deadline to file a retaliation complaint under Labor Code 6310. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar your claim.

Through workers' compensation, you can recover medical treatment costs, temporary and permanent disability benefits, supplemental job displacement benefits, and death benefits for dependents. Through a third-party liability claim, you can recover the full range of personal injury damages: medical expenses (past and future), lost wages and earning capacity, pain and suffering, emotional distress, and loss of consortium. Third-party claims often result in significantly larger recoveries than workers' comp alone.
