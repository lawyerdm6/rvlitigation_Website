Why Truck Accidents Are Different

A collision with a commercial truck is not like a typical car accident. A fully loaded 18-wheeler can weigh up to 80,000 pounds — roughly 20 times the weight of a passenger vehicle. When these massive vehicles collide with cars on Bay Area highways like I-680, US-101, and I-280, the results are often catastrophic: traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, crushed limbs, and fatalities.

Truck accident cases are also far more legally complex. They involve federal FMCSA regulations, multiple potentially liable parties — the driver, the trucking company, the cargo loader, the vehicle manufacturer — and insurance policies that can exceed $1 million. Trucking companies dispatch rapid-response teams to accident scenes within hours, working to limit their exposure before victims even leave the hospital. If you or a family member has been injured in a truck accident in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in Santa Clara County, contact the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC immediately.

What the Law Says

49 CFR 395 — FMCSA Hours-of-Service Rules

"A driver of a property-carrying commercial motor vehicle may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty. A driver may not drive beyond the 14th consecutive hour after coming on duty, following 10 consecutive hours off duty." — 49 CFR § 395.3 (Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations)

The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) sets strict hours-of-service limits to prevent fatigued driving. Truck drivers are limited to 11 hours of driving within a 14-hour window after 10 consecutive hours off duty. They must also take a mandatory 30-minute break after 8 cumulative hours of driving. Despite these regulations, trucking companies routinely pressure drivers to push past these limits to meet delivery deadlines. When they do, they put every driver on the road at risk. Violations of HOS rules are strong evidence of negligence in truck accident cases throughout the Bay Area and across California.

California Vehicle Code 35551 — Weight Limits

"The total gross weight in pounds imposed on the highway by any group of two or more consecutive axles shall not exceed that given for the respective distances in the following table..." — California Vehicle Code § 35551

California law sets maximum weight limits for commercial vehicles operating on state highways. The federal gross vehicle weight limit is 80,000 pounds, but California may impose lower axle-weight limits depending on road conditions and bridge ratings. Overloaded trucks require greater stopping distances, are more prone to tire blowouts, and are harder to control during emergency maneuvers. When a truck is overloaded and causes a crash on highways like I-5 through Gilroy or US-101 through San Jose, the trucking company and the entity responsible for loading the cargo may both be held liable.

California Civil Code 1714 — General Negligence

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person..." — California Civil Code § 1714(a)

California's general negligence statute provides the foundation for most truck accident injury claims. To recover compensation, you must show that the defendant owed you a duty of care, breached that duty, and that the breach caused your injuries. In truck accident cases, this duty extends beyond the driver to include the trucking company (through vicarious liability and negligent hiring), maintenance contractors, cargo loaders, and equipment manufacturers. California's comparative negligence system means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault — your award is simply reduced by your percentage of responsibility.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how truck accident claims commonly arise on Bay Area roads:

Example 1 — Jackknife on I-680

A tractor-trailer traveling southbound on I-680 near Milpitas in wet conditions jackknifes across three lanes, striking two passenger vehicles. Investigation reveals the truck driver had been driving for 13 consecutive hours in violation of FMCSA hours-of-service regulations, and the trucking company's electronic logging device (ELD) records had been falsified. Both the driver and the company face liability for the injuries sustained.

Example 2 — Tire Blowout on US-101

A big rig heading northbound on US-101 through San Jose suffers a catastrophic tire blowout, sending debris into oncoming traffic and causing a multi-vehicle collision. Maintenance records show the truck's tires had not been inspected in over six months, violating FMCSA pre-trip inspection requirements under 49 CFR 396. The tire manufacturer, the maintenance company, and the trucking company may all bear responsibility.

Example 3 — Loading Dock Accident in San Jose

A warehouse worker in San Jose is crushed between a dock and a reversing semi-truck when the driver fails to check mirrors and ignores a spotter's signals. The investigation reveals the driver had no valid commercial driver's license (CDL) for the vehicle class, and the trucking company failed to verify the driver's qualifications — a violation of 49 CFR 391 (driver qualification requirements). Claims are filed against the driver, the trucking company, and the warehouse operator.

Example 4 — Drowsy Driver on I-5

A commercial truck driver traveling on I-5 through southern Santa Clara County near Gilroy drifts across the center line at 3:00 AM and strikes a passenger car head-on, killing the other driver. The truck's black box data shows no braking before impact, and the driver's logbook reveals he had been on duty for 16 straight hours. The victim's family files a wrongful death claim against the driver and the trucking company, seeking both compensatory and punitive damages for the company's willful disregard of federal safety rules.

What's at Stake

Truck accident injuries are often severe and life-altering. California law allows victims to pursue several categories of damages:

Damage Category Description Typical Range
Medical Expenses Emergency care, surgery, hospitalization, rehabilitation, future treatment $50,000 – $2,000,000+
Lost Wages Past and future lost income, diminished earning capacity $25,000 – $1,000,000+
Pain & Suffering Physical pain, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life Varies widely by severity
Property Damage Vehicle repair or replacement, personal property $5,000 – $100,000+
Punitive Damages Awarded when conduct is fraudulent, oppressive, or malicious (CC 3294) No statutory cap
Wrongful Death Funeral costs, loss of financial support, loss of companionship (CCP 377.60) $500,000 – $10,000,000+

Important: California does not cap non-economic damages in personal injury cases (unlike medical malpractice). Truck accident claims often involve insurance policies of $1 million or more, giving victims access to meaningful compensation — but only if they build a strong case with proper evidence preservation, expert testimony, and aggressive litigation when needed.

How We Help

Truck accident cases require immediate action and specialized legal knowledge. Here is how the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC fight for our clients in San Jose and throughout the Bay Area:

1. Investigating Black Box Data

Every commercial truck is equipped with an Electronic Control Module (ECM) that records critical data — speed at the time of impact, braking patterns, throttle position, engine hours, and more. This is often the most powerful piece of evidence in a truck accident case. However, trucking companies can overwrite or destroy this data within days. We send immediate spoliation preservation letters to the trucking company, driver, and insurance carrier, demanding that all ECM data, dashcam footage, GPS records, and electronic logging device (ELD) data be preserved. If they destroy evidence, we seek sanctions and adverse inference jury instructions.

2. Identifying FMCSA Violations

Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration regulations are extensive and technical. We audit the trucking company's compliance with hours-of-service rules (49 CFR 395), driver qualification files (49 CFR 391), vehicle maintenance and inspection requirements (49 CFR 396), drug and alcohol testing programs (49 CFR 382), and cargo securement standards (49 CFR 393). A single violation can shift liability decisively to the trucking company. We also check the company's safety rating and crash history through the FMCSA's Safety Measurement System (SMS) database.

3. Pursuing Multiple Liable Parties

Unlike a typical car accident where only one driver is at fault, truck accident cases often involve a chain of negligent parties. The truck driver may have been fatigued or impaired. The trucking company may have pressured the driver to skip rest breaks or failed to maintain the vehicle. The cargo loading company may have overloaded or improperly secured the freight. The truck or parts manufacturer may have sold a defective brake system, tire, or coupling device. The maintenance contractor may have cut corners on inspections. We investigate every link in the chain to identify all responsible parties and maximize the total recovery.

4. Defeating Insurance Company Tactics

Trucking companies carry large insurance policies — often $1 million to $5 million or more — and their insurers deploy aggressive strategies to minimize payouts. They will send investigators to the scene before the ambulance leaves. They will request recorded statements designed to be used against you. They will offer quick, lowball settlements while you are still in pain and unsure of your future medical needs. We handle all communications with insurance companies, reject inadequate offers, and build cases that demonstrate the full extent of our clients' injuries and losses.

5. Retaining Expert Witnesses

Truck accident cases frequently require expert testimony to establish liability and damages. We work with accident reconstruction engineers who analyze skid marks, vehicle damage, and road conditions to determine how the crash occurred. We retain trucking industry experts who can testify about industry standards and FMCSA compliance. We engage medical specialists to document the full extent of injuries and project future treatment needs. And we use economic experts to calculate lifetime lost earnings and the cost of ongoing care for clients throughout San Jose, San Francisco, Morgan Hill, and the greater Bay Area.

6. Trial Preparation

Most truck accident cases settle, but the best settlements come when the insurance company knows you are prepared to go to trial. We prepare every case as if it will be tried before a jury in Santa Clara County Superior Court or the United States District Court for the Northern District of California. That means thorough discovery, strong depositions of the driver and company representatives, compelling demonstrative exhibits, and a damages presentation that communicates the true human cost of the collision. If the trucking company refuses to offer fair compensation, we take the case to trial.
