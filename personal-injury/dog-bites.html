Understanding Dog Bite Claims in California

Dog bites cause more than physical pain. Victims frequently face emergency room bills, surgical procedures, permanent scarring, nerve damage, infections, and lasting psychological trauma. Children are especially vulnerable, often suffering bites to the face and head that require reconstructive surgery. In California, the law is firmly on the victim's side.

Under Civil Code 3342, California imposes strict liability on dog owners. This means you do not need to prove that the owner was negligent or that the dog had a history of aggression. If the dog bit you and you were lawfully present at the location, the owner is liable. Period. If you or a loved one has been bitten by a dog in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in Santa Clara County, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can help you recover the full compensation you deserve.

What the Law Says

Civil Code 3342 — Strict Liability for Dog Bites

"The owner of any dog is liable for the damages suffered by any person who is bitten by the dog while in a public place or lawfully in a private place, including the property of the owner of the dog, regardless of the former viciousness of the dog or the owner's knowledge of such viciousness." — California Civil Code Section 3342(a)

This statute is the foundation of dog bite claims in California. Unlike many other states that follow a "one-bite rule" — where the owner is only liable if they knew the dog was dangerous — California holds owners responsible for any bite, even the first one. The key requirements are straightforward: (1) the defendant owned the dog, (2) the dog bit the plaintiff, and (3) the plaintiff was in a public place or lawfully in a private place when bitten. There is no requirement to prove negligence, prior knowledge of aggression, or failure to restrain the animal.

Civil Code 3342.5 — Dangerous Dog Designation

"Whenever a dog has bitten a human being on at least two separate occasions, any person, the district attorney, or city attorney may bring an action against the owner of the dog to determine whether conditions of the dog's treatment or confinement are such that it presents a danger to the public." — California Civil Code Section 3342.5

This section provides an additional layer of protection for the community. If a dog has bitten people on two or more occasions, a court may impose conditions on how the dog must be kept or, in extreme cases, order the dog to be removed from the area or destroyed. For victims, a dangerous dog designation strengthens a claim by establishing a documented pattern of dangerous behavior. If the owner was previously put on notice under CC 3342.5 and failed to take adequate precautions, this can support claims for enhanced damages.

Local Leash Laws and Municipal Ordinances

In addition to state statutes, Bay Area cities enforce local leash laws that require dogs to be restrained in public. San Jose Municipal Code Chapter 7.08 requires dogs to be on a leash no longer than six feet in public areas. San Francisco's Health Code Article 1, Section 41 mandates leash control in most public spaces. Violations of these ordinances can serve as evidence of negligence per se in a dog bite case, meaning the owner's violation of the law is automatically treated as negligence — no additional proof of carelessness is needed.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how dog bite claims commonly arise in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Unleashed Dog at a San Jose Park

A family is having a picnic at Guadalupe River Park in San Jose when an off-leash pit bull runs toward them and bites a seven-year-old child on the arm and face. The dog owner claims the dog "has never done this before." Under CC 3342 strict liability, it does not matter. The owner is liable for all medical expenses, plastic surgery, pain and suffering, and the child's emotional trauma — regardless of the dog's prior history.

Example 2 — Delivery Worker Bitten on Duty

A package delivery driver approaches a home in Morgan Hill to drop off a parcel. The homeowner's German shepherd escapes through the front door and bites the driver on the leg, requiring stitches and a course of antibiotics. The driver was lawfully on the property performing a job duty. The homeowner's insurance policy covers the claim, and the driver recovers compensation for medical bills, lost wages during recovery, and pain and suffering.

Example 3 — Child Attacked at Neighbor's Home

A ten-year-old is invited to play at a friend's house in Gilroy. The family's dog, which has no bite history, suddenly lunges and bites the child on the hand, severing a tendon. Because the child was lawfully in a private place — she was an invited guest — strict liability applies. The neighbor's homeowner's insurance covers the medical bills, hand surgery, occupational therapy, and compensation for the child's scarring and fear of dogs.

Example 4 — Jogger Bitten on a Trail

A woman jogging along the Los Gatos Creek Trail in San Jose is bitten on the calf by a dog whose owner lost control of the retractable leash. The bite causes a deep puncture wound that becomes infected, requiring two rounds of antibiotics and leaving a permanent scar. The jogger was in a public place and did nothing to provoke the animal. She files a claim for emergency room costs, follow-up care, scarring damages, and lost income from missed work.

What's at Stake

Dog bite injuries can result in significant damages. The compensation available depends on the severity of the injury, the circumstances of the attack, and the long-term impact on the victim's life.

Type of Damages Description Common Range
Medical Expenses ER visits, surgery, stitches, antibiotics, rabies treatment, plastic/reconstructive surgery $5,000 – $250,000+
Lost Wages Income lost during recovery; reduced earning capacity for permanent injuries Varies
Pain & Suffering Physical pain, emotional distress, anxiety, PTSD, fear of dogs (cynophobia) $10,000 – $500,000+
Scarring & Disfigurement Permanent scarring, especially facial injuries; disfigurement requiring cosmetic procedures $15,000 – $1,000,000+
Property Damage Damaged clothing, personal items, eyeglasses, or electronics during the attack $100 – $5,000
Punitive Damages Available when the owner's conduct was willful, malicious, or grossly negligent (e.g., known vicious dog kept unrestrained) Case-dependent

Important: California follows a pure comparative negligence standard. Even if the defense argues you were partially at fault — for example, by approaching the dog without the owner's permission — your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault, not eliminated. Additionally, homeowner's and renter's insurance policies typically cover dog bite liability, meaning there is usually a viable source of compensation available even when the dog owner has limited personal assets.

How We Help

Dog bite cases require prompt action. Evidence disappears, witnesses forget details, and medical records must be carefully documented from day one. Our attorneys handle every aspect of your claim so you can focus on recovery.

1. Thorough Case Investigation

We immediately investigate the circumstances of the attack. This includes photographing your injuries, identifying the dog and its owner, obtaining animal control records, checking whether the dog has a history of complaints or prior bites under CC 3342.5, and securing any surveillance footage from nearby homes or businesses. In San Jose and surrounding cities, we also pull municipal code violation records to determine whether the owner was cited for leash law violations.

2. Documenting Your Injuries and Damages

We work with your medical providers to ensure every injury is properly documented — from the initial emergency room visit through follow-up care, physical therapy, plastic surgery consultations, and psychological counseling. We also calculate lost wages, reduced earning capacity, and the ongoing cost of any future medical treatment you may need. Thorough documentation is what separates adequate settlements from full-value recoveries.

3. Identifying All Sources of Compensation

Most dog bite claims are paid through the owner's homeowner's or renter's insurance. We identify every applicable policy, including umbrella coverage, and determine the policy limits. If the dog was in the care of a dog walker, kennel, pet sitter, or landlord at the time of the attack, additional parties may share liability. We pursue every available source of recovery to maximize your compensation.

4. Aggressive Negotiation with Insurance Companies

Insurance companies routinely undervalue dog bite claims. They may argue that your injuries were pre-existing, that you provoked the dog, or that your medical treatment was excessive. Our attorneys know these tactics and counter them with medical evidence, expert opinions, and a clear presentation of your damages. We negotiate from a position of strength, backed by the willingness to go to trial if a fair settlement is not offered.

5. Protecting Children's Interests

Children are the most common victims of dog bites and often suffer the most severe injuries, including facial lacerations and lasting psychological trauma. When a minor is involved, we take extra steps to ensure the settlement or verdict is structured to cover future medical needs, therapy, and potential cosmetic procedures as the child grows. California law requires court approval of minor settlements to ensure the child's interests are protected.

6. Filing Suit and Trial Preparation

If the insurance company refuses to offer fair compensation, we file a lawsuit in Santa Clara County Superior Court or the appropriate jurisdiction and prepare your case for trial. Our litigation team handles discovery, depositions, expert witness coordination, and courtroom presentation. Most cases settle before trial, but having a firm that is genuinely prepared to try the case is what drives fair settlement offers.
