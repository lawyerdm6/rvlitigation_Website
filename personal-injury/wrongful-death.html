Understanding Wrongful Death Claims in California

The sudden, preventable death of a loved one is among the most devastating experiences a family can endure. When someone dies because of another person's or entity's negligence, recklessness, or intentional misconduct, California law provides surviving family members with the right to file a wrongful death claim to seek financial compensation for their losses. These claims exist not only to help families cope with the financial aftermath but to hold wrongdoers accountable.

What the Law Says

CCP 377.60 — Wrongful Death

"A cause of action for the death of a person caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another may be asserted by any of the following persons or by the decedent's personal representative on their behalf: (a) The decedent's surviving spouse, domestic partner, children, and issue of deceased children... (b) Whether or not qualified under subdivision (a), if they were dependent on the decedent..." — California Code of Civil Procedure Section 377.60

CCP 377.60 is the foundation of California's wrongful death statute. It allows certain surviving family members to bring a civil lawsuit when a person dies as a result of someone else's wrongful act or neglect. The law defines who may file the claim and establishes that compensation is available for the losses suffered by the survivors themselves — not the deceased. The statute encompasses deaths caused by negligence, recklessness, intentional acts, product defects, medical errors, and other forms of misconduct.

CCP 377.30 — Survival Actions

"A cause of action that survives the death of the person entitled to commence an action or proceeding passes to the decedent's successor in interest... and an action may be commenced by the decedent's personal representative or, if none, by the decedent's successor in interest." — California Code of Civil Procedure Section 377.30

A survival action is a separate but related claim that belongs to the deceased person's estate. While a wrongful death claim compensates surviving family members for their own losses, a survival action recovers damages the deceased person experienced before death — including pain and suffering endured between the moment of injury and the moment of death, medical expenses incurred during that period, and lost wages. In cases where the victim survived for any period of time before dying, a survival action can significantly increase the total recovery.

Civil Code 1714 — General Negligence

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person..." — California Civil Code Section 1714

Civil Code 1714 establishes the general duty of care that every person in California owes to others. This statute underpins most wrongful death claims by establishing that when a party fails to exercise ordinary care — whether a driver, a property owner, a doctor, or a manufacturer — and that failure results in someone's death, the responsible party can be held liable. The duty of care applies to individuals, businesses, government entities, and other organizations.

Common Wrongful Death Scenarios

Wrongful death claims arise from a wide range of circumstances. The following examples reflect situations our attorneys commonly encounter in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Fatal Car Crash on Highway 101

A distracted driver on Highway 101 near San Jose rear-ends a vehicle at high speed, killing the driver. The deceased was a 38-year-old parent and primary breadwinner. The surviving spouse and two children file a wrongful death claim seeking compensation for lost income that would have supported the family for decades, loss of parental guidance, funeral costs, and the companionship they will never have again. An investigation reveals the at-fault driver was texting at the time of impact.

Example 2 — Workplace Fatality in Construction

A construction worker in Santa Clara County is killed when a scaffolding system collapses due to improper assembly. While workers' compensation covers some benefits, it does not provide full compensation for the family's loss. The surviving family brings a wrongful death claim against the general contractor and scaffolding subcontractor for failing to follow OSHA safety standards. The claim is separate from workers' compensation and can recover non-economic damages that workers' comp does not cover.

Example 3 — Medical Malpractice Death

A 65-year-old patient in San Francisco undergoes a routine surgical procedure. The surgeon fails to identify and control internal bleeding during the operation. The patient dies two days later from complications that should have been caught. The family files a wrongful death claim against the surgeon and hospital, along with a survival action for the pain and suffering the patient experienced during those final hours. Expert medical testimony confirms the standard of care was not met.

Example 4 — Pedestrian Killed in a Crosswalk

An elderly pedestrian is struck and killed by a delivery truck while crossing at a marked crosswalk in Morgan Hill. The truck driver failed to yield the right of way. The deceased's adult children file a wrongful death claim against the driver and the trucking company, which is held vicariously liable under the doctrine of respondeat superior. Investigation reveals the driver had been on duty for 14 hours in violation of federal hours-of-service regulations, and the company had a pattern of inadequate driver supervision.

What's at Stake — Recoverable Damages

California law allows surviving family members to recover both economic and non-economic damages in a wrongful death case. There is no statutory cap on damages in most wrongful death claims (medical malpractice cases have separate rules under MICRA). The following table outlines the categories of compensation available:

Damage Category Description Claim Type
Funeral & Burial Costs Reasonable expenses for funeral services, burial or cremation, memorial Wrongful Death
Lost Financial Support Present value of income, benefits, and financial contributions the deceased would have provided Wrongful Death
Loss of Companionship Loss of love, affection, comfort, care, society, moral support, and guidance Wrongful Death
Pain & Suffering Before Death Physical pain and emotional distress the deceased experienced between injury and death Survival Action
Punitive Damages Available in survival actions when the defendant acted with malice, oppression, or fraud Survival Action

Important considerations: Economic damages are calculated using the deceased's age, health, earning capacity, life expectancy, and the financial needs of dependents. Non-economic damages — while more difficult to quantify — often represent the largest portion of a wrongful death award. In cases involving egregious misconduct, a survival action may include punitive damages designed to punish the wrongdoer and deter similar conduct. Our attorneys work with forensic economists, vocational experts, and life-care planners to build the strongest possible case for your family.

How We Help

Wrongful death cases require a unique combination of legal expertise, investigative resources, and genuine compassion. Here is how our attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC advocate for grieving families throughout San Jose, San Francisco, and the Bay Area:

1. Identifying All Liable Parties

In many wrongful death cases, more than one party bears responsibility. A fatal car accident may involve a negligent driver, a vehicle manufacturer with a defective product, and a government entity responsible for dangerous road design. A workplace death may implicate a general contractor, subcontractors, equipment manufacturers, and property owners. We conduct a thorough investigation to identify every potentially liable party, which maximizes the sources of compensation available to your family and ensures that no responsible party escapes accountability.

2. Calculating Lifetime Damages

Accurately calculating the full financial impact of a wrongful death is critical to achieving a fair result. We work with forensic economists who analyze the deceased's earning history, career trajectory, benefits, pension contributions, and projected lifetime earnings. We also account for the value of household services, parental guidance, and other non-monetary contributions. For families in high-cost areas like San Jose and San Francisco, these calculations must reflect the true cost of living in the Bay Area. Our goal is to ensure that the compensation reflects the full scope of what your family has lost.

3. Navigating Family Dynamics

Wrongful death claims can involve multiple family members with different relationships to the deceased and different claims to compensation. A surviving spouse, adult children, minor children, and dependent parents may all have standing to participate in the case. Disagreements about how to proceed or how to divide a recovery can complicate the process. Our attorneys have experience guiding families through these sensitive discussions, ensuring that everyone's interests are respected while keeping the case moving forward effectively.

4. Retaining Expert Economists and Medical Professionals

Strong wrongful death cases are built on strong evidence, and expert testimony is often the backbone of that evidence. We retain board-certified medical experts to establish the cause and manner of death, accident reconstruction specialists to demonstrate how the incident occurred, and vocational economists to project future lost earnings. In medical malpractice wrongful death cases, we work with physicians in the same specialty as the defendant to establish that the standard of care was breached. These experts strengthen your case at the negotiating table and in the courtroom.

5. Aggressive Insurance Negotiations

Insurance companies routinely attempt to minimize wrongful death payouts. They may dispute liability, question the extent of the family's financial dependence on the deceased, or pressure families into accepting lowball settlements during a period of extreme vulnerability. We do not allow that to happen. Our attorneys handle all communications with insurers, present detailed demand packages supported by expert analysis, and negotiate aggressively to achieve settlements that truly reflect the value of the case. If the insurer refuses to offer fair compensation, we are fully prepared to take the case to trial.

6. Compassionate Trial Advocacy

Not every wrongful death case settles. When a defendant or insurer refuses to accept responsibility or offer fair compensation, we take the case to a jury. Trial advocacy in wrongful death cases requires a delicate balance — presenting the devastating impact of the loss in a way that resonates with jurors while maintaining the dignity and privacy of the family. Our attorneys have courtroom experience presenting these cases to juries throughout Santa Clara County, San Francisco, and across the Bay Area, and we approach every trial with the respect and sensitivity your family deserves.
