What Is Medical Malpractice?

Medical malpractice occurs when a healthcare provider — a doctor, surgeon, nurse, hospital, or other medical professional — fails to meet the accepted standard of care, and that failure directly causes injury to a patient. In California, medical malpractice claims are governed by a complex set of statutes, including CCP 340.5 (statute of limitations), MICRA (the Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act), and Business & Professions Code 6146 (attorney fee limits).

What the Law Says

CCP 340.5 — Statute of Limitations & Discovery Rule

"In an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person's alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first." — California Code of Civil Procedure Section 340.5

California's statute of limitations for medical malpractice is among the most restrictive in the country. Under CCP 340.5, you have the earlier of: one year from discovery of the injury or three years from the date the injury occurred. The discovery rule is critical — the clock does not start running until you knew or reasonably should have known that your injury was caused by medical negligence. This is particularly important in cases involving retained surgical instruments, delayed diagnosis of cancer, or other conditions where the harm may not be immediately apparent.

There are exceptions for minors (children under six have until their eighth birthday), fraud or concealment by the provider (which tolls the statute), and cases involving foreign objects left in the body (the one-year discovery period applies from the date of discovery, without the three-year outer limit).

Business & Professions Code 6146 — Attorney Fee Limits

"An attorney shall not contract for or collect a contingency fee for representing any person seeking damages in connection with an action for injury or damage against a health care provider based upon such person's alleged professional negligence in excess of the following limits: (a) Forty percent of the first fifty thousand dollars ($50,000) recovered; (b) Thirty-three and one-third percent of the next fifty thousand dollars ($50,000) recovered; (c) Twenty-five percent of the next five hundred thousand dollars ($500,000) recovered; (d) Fifteen percent of any amount on which the recovery exceeds six hundred thousand dollars ($600,000)." — California Business & Professions Code Section 6146

Unlike other personal injury cases where attorneys typically charge a flat 33–40% contingency fee, California law caps attorney fees in medical malpractice cases on a sliding scale. This is another MICRA provision designed to limit the costs of medical malpractice litigation. While this means attorneys receive a smaller percentage as the recovery increases, it also means your attorney must be highly selective and confident in the merits of your case before agreeing to represent you.

MICRA (CCP 3333.2) — Damages Cap & AB 35 Changes

"In any action for injury against a health care provider based on professional negligence, the injured plaintiff shall be entitled to recover noneconomic losses to compensate for pain, suffering, inconvenience, physical impairment, disfigurement and other nonpecuniary damage." — California Civil Code Section 3333.2 (as amended by AB 35)

Since 1975, California's MICRA law capped non-economic damages (pain and suffering) in medical malpractice cases at just $250,000 — a figure that had not been adjusted for inflation in nearly 50 years. In 2022, the legislature passed AB 35, which took effect January 1, 2023, making significant changes:

For cases not involving death: The cap increased from $250,000 to $350,000, and will increase by $40,000 per year until reaching $750,000 in 2033.

For wrongful death cases: The cap started at $500,000 and increases by $50,000 per year until reaching $1,000,000 in 2033.

After 2033, the caps will be adjusted for inflation. Importantly, MICRA only caps non-economic damages. There is no cap on economic damages such as medical bills, lost wages, and future care costs. Understanding how to maximize your total recovery within these constraints is one of the most important skills a medical malpractice attorney must have.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how medical malpractice claims commonly arise in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Surgical Error

A patient undergoes knee surgery at a San Jose hospital, but the surgeon operates on the wrong knee. The patient must undergo a second surgery on the correct knee, suffering additional pain, extended recovery time, and significant medical costs. This is a clear case of medical malpractice — the surgeon failed to follow basic surgical safety protocols, including the required pre-operative verification and surgical site marking under the Universal Protocol.

Example 2 — Delayed Cancer Diagnosis

A woman in Santa Clara County visits her primary care physician complaining of persistent abdominal pain and bloating. The doctor attributes her symptoms to stress and IBS without ordering imaging or further testing. Eighteen months later, she is diagnosed with Stage III ovarian cancer. Had her doctor ordered a CT scan or referred her to a specialist at the initial visit, the cancer could have been caught at Stage I, dramatically improving her prognosis and survival rate.

Example 3 — ER Misdiagnosis

A man presents to an emergency room in San Francisco with severe chest pain, shortness of breath, and sweating. The ER physician diagnoses him with acid reflux and sends him home with antacids. Two days later, he suffers a massive heart attack that causes permanent cardiac damage. The failure to order an EKG, cardiac enzymes, or other standard cardiac workup in a patient presenting with classic heart attack symptoms constitutes a breach of the standard of care.

Example 4 — Birth Injury

During a complicated labor at a Morgan Hill birthing center, the obstetrician fails to perform a timely cesarean section despite signs of fetal distress, including abnormal heart rate tracings on the electronic fetal monitor. The prolonged oxygen deprivation causes the infant to develop cerebral palsy. The lifetime cost of care for a child with cerebral palsy can exceed several million dollars — and MICRA does not cap these economic damages.

What's at Stake

Medical malpractice damages in California fall into several categories. Understanding the distinction between economic and non-economic damages is essential because of MICRA's cap on non-economic recovery.

Damage Type Category Description Cap
Past & Future Medical Costs Economic Hospital bills, surgeries, rehab, medications, future care No cap
Lost Income & Earning Capacity Economic Wages lost during recovery, diminished future earning ability No cap
Pain & Suffering Non-Economic Physical pain, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life MICRA cap applies
Loss of Consortium Non-Economic Spouse's claim for loss of companionship and intimacy MICRA cap applies
Punitive Damages Punitive Available only if provider acted with fraud, oppression, or malice No cap (rare)

Important: The MICRA cap on non-economic damages currently stands at $350,000 for non-death cases (increasing $40,000/year) and $500,000 for wrongful death cases (increasing $50,000/year). Each plaintiff in a wrongful death action gets their own cap — meaning if a spouse and two children each file claims, each can recover up to the cap amount. Economic damages — medical bills, lost wages, and future care costs — have no cap whatsoever, which is why thorough documentation of every economic loss is critical to maximizing your recovery.

How We Help

Medical malpractice cases are among the most complex in personal injury law. Here is how the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC build and pursue your claim:

1. Medical Expert Review

Every medical malpractice case in California requires expert testimony. Before we file a claim, we retain board-certified medical experts in the relevant specialty to review your medical records. These experts evaluate whether the standard of care was breached and whether that breach caused your injury. We work with specialists in surgery, oncology, obstetrics, emergency medicine, cardiology, and other fields to build a case supported by the highest level of medical evidence.

2. Standard of Care Analysis

The central question in every medical malpractice case is whether the provider met the standard of care — the level of treatment a competent provider in the same specialty would have provided under similar circumstances. We conduct a thorough analysis of your treatment, comparing it against published medical guidelines, hospital protocols, and the opinions of our retained experts to identify exactly where the care you received fell short.

3. MICRA Navigation

California's MICRA framework creates unique challenges that do not exist in other personal injury cases. Our attorneys understand how to maximize your total recovery within MICRA's constraints — by thoroughly documenting every economic loss (which has no cap), pursuing per-plaintiff caps in wrongful death cases, and presenting non-economic damages in the most compelling way possible. We also evaluate whether your case involves conduct that falls outside MICRA's protections, such as fraud or intentional misconduct.

4. Pre-Suit Requirements

Before filing a medical malpractice lawsuit in California, you must comply with the 90-day notice requirement under CCP 364. This notice informs the healthcare provider of your intent to file a claim and tolls (pauses) the statute of limitations for 90 days. Failure to send proper notice can result in your case being dismissed. We handle all pre-suit requirements meticulously, ensuring every procedural step is completed correctly and on time.

5. Hospital & Insurance Negotiations

Most medical malpractice cases involve negotiations with hospital risk management departments and medical malpractice insurance carriers — entities with vast resources and experienced defense teams. We negotiate aggressively on your behalf, backed by strong expert opinions and thorough documentation. Our goal is to secure the maximum possible settlement, but we never recommend accepting an offer that does not fully compensate you for your injuries.

6. Trial Preparation & Litigation

If the defense refuses to offer fair compensation, we take your case to trial. Medical malpractice trials require presenting complex medical evidence in a way that a jury can understand and connect with. We work with medical illustrators, life care planners, and economists to present the full scope of your damages. Our trial attorneys have the courtroom experience to advocate effectively before judges and juries in Santa Clara County Superior Court, San Francisco Superior Court, and courts throughout the Bay Area.

Related Practice Areas

Wrongful Death

CCP 377.60 — When medical negligence results in the death of a patient, surviving family members may pursue a wrongful death claim.

Workplace Injuries

Injuries sustained at work that are worsened by negligent medical treatment may give rise to both workers' comp and malpractice claims.

Dog Bites

CC 3342 — Dog bite injuries requiring surgery or reconstructive care that is negligently performed may involve both liability and malpractice claims.

Car Accidents

Auto accident victims who receive negligent medical treatment may have claims against both the at-fault driver and the healthcare provider.
