Bicycle Accidents in California

California has more bicyclists on the road than any other state, and the Bay Area is one of the most active cycling regions in the country. Unfortunately, the combination of heavy traffic, narrow lanes, and distracted driving makes San Jose, San Francisco, and surrounding communities some of the most dangerous places to ride. According to the California Office of Traffic Safety, thousands of cyclists are injured in collisions with motor vehicles every year across the state.

When a car, truck, or bus strikes a cyclist, the injuries are almost always serious. Bicyclists have no seatbelts, no airbags, and virtually no structural protection. Broken bones, traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, and road rash are common outcomes. If you or a loved one was injured in a bicycle accident in Santa Clara County, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in the Bay Area, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can help you recover the compensation you deserve.

What the Law Says

CVC 21200 — Bicycles as Vehicles

"Every person riding a bicycle upon a highway has all the rights and is subject to all the provisions applicable to the driver of a vehicle... except those provisions which by their very nature can have no application." — California Vehicle Code Section 21200

This is the foundational statute for bicycle law in California. Under CVC 21200, a bicycle is legally a vehicle, and a cyclist has the same rights as any driver on the road. This means motorists must yield to cyclists in the same situations they would yield to other vehicles, and a driver who strikes a cyclist can be held liable under the same negligence standards that apply to car-on-car collisions. The statute cuts both ways: cyclists must also obey traffic signals, stop signs, and lane-use rules. However, a cyclist's minor traffic violation does not excuse a motorist's negligence that caused the collision.

CVC 21760 — Three-Foot Passing Law

"The driver of a motor vehicle overtaking and passing a bicycle that is proceeding in the same direction on a highway shall... pass to the left at a safe distance that does not interfere with the safe operation of the overtaken bicycle, having due regard for the size and speed of the motor vehicle and the bicycle, traffic conditions, weather, visibility, and the surface and width of the highway." — California Vehicle Code Section 21760(c)

California's three-foot passing law requires every motorist to maintain at least three feet of clearance when overtaking a bicyclist. If road conditions prevent a three-foot gap, the driver must slow down to a safe speed and wait until passing is safe. A violation of CVC 21760 that causes bodily injury to a cyclist carries an additional $220 fine. More importantly for civil claims, a violation of this statute is strong evidence of negligence and can significantly strengthen your personal injury case.

CVC 21208 — Bicycle Lane Use

"Whenever a bicycle lane has been established on a roadway... any person operating a bicycle upon the roadway at a speed less than the normal speed of traffic... shall ride within the bicycle lane, except... when reasonably necessary to leave the bicycle lane to avoid debris or other hazardous conditions." — California Vehicle Code Section 21208(a)

While cyclists are generally required to use a designated bike lane when one is available, the law provides important exceptions. A cyclist may leave the bike lane to make a left turn, to avoid debris, parked cars, pedestrians, or other hazards, or when approaching a right-turn-only lane. A motorist who argues that a cyclist was "out of the bike lane" often misunderstands this statute. Our attorneys know how to counter this defense and demonstrate that the cyclist was riding lawfully.

Civil Code 1714 — General Negligence

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person..." — California Civil Code Section 1714(a)

This is the general negligence statute that forms the basis for most bicycle accident injury claims in California. Under CC 1714, a driver who fails to exercise ordinary care — by texting while driving, failing to check mirrors before opening a car door, running a red light, or making an unsafe turn — is legally responsible for the injuries that result. Combined with the specific protections of CVC 21200 and CVC 21760, this statute gives injured cyclists a strong legal foundation for recovering damages.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios reflect the types of bicycle accident cases our attorneys handle throughout the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Dooring on Market Street, San Francisco

A cyclist is riding in the bike lane on Market Street in San Francisco during the evening commute. A rideshare passenger opens the rear door of a parked vehicle without checking for approaching traffic. The cyclist collides with the door at speed, is thrown from the bike, and suffers a broken collarbone, facial lacerations, and a concussion. Both the passenger who opened the door and the driver who failed to warn the passenger may be liable under CVC 22517, which requires any person opening a vehicle door to first ensure it is safe to do so.

Example 2 — Right-Hook at Intersection, San Jose

A driver traveling eastbound on Santa Clara Street in downtown San Jose makes a right turn onto a cross street, cutting directly across the bike lane without yielding to a cyclist who has the right of way. The cyclist T-bones the turning vehicle and sustains a shattered wrist, torn rotator cuff, and multiple fractured ribs. The driver violated CVC 21717, which requires motorists to merge safely into the bike lane before turning right, and CVC 21200, which grants cyclists the same right-of-way as motor vehicles.

Example 3 — Road Hazard on Stevens Creek Trail

A recreational cyclist is riding the Stevens Creek Trail in Mountain View when they hit an unmarked section of severely cracked and uneven pavement where tree roots have buckled the trail surface. The cyclist loses control, crashes, and suffers a fractured pelvis and herniated disc. Because the trail is maintained by the City, a government tort claim may be filed if the municipality knew about the dangerous condition and failed to repair or warn cyclists. Under Government Code 835, a public entity can be held liable for a dangerous condition of public property.

Example 4 — Hit by Distracted Driver on El Camino Real

A cyclist riding lawfully in the right lane on El Camino Real in Sunnyvale is struck from behind by a driver who was looking at a phone. The impact throws the cyclist more than 20 feet. The cyclist suffers a traumatic brain injury, multiple spinal fractures, and road rash requiring skin grafts. The driver violated CVC 23123.5 (handheld phone use while driving), CVC 21760 (failure to maintain three feet of clearance), and the general duty of care under CC 1714. Cases involving distracted driving often result in substantial damage awards because the driver's conduct is particularly egregious.

What's at Stake

Bicycle accident injuries are frequently severe and life-altering. California law allows injured cyclists to recover both economic and non-economic damages. The value of your claim depends on the severity of your injuries, the extent of your financial losses, and the impact on your quality of life.

Damage Category Description Examples
Medical Expenses Past, current, and future medical costs ER visits, surgery, rehab, prosthetics, TBI treatment
Lost Wages Income lost during recovery Missed work, reduced hours, inability to perform job duties
Loss of Earning Capacity Future income reduction from permanent disability Career change, early retirement, permanent work restrictions
Pain & Suffering Physical pain and emotional distress Chronic pain, PTSD, anxiety, depression, loss of enjoyment
Property Damage Damage or destruction of bicycle and gear Bicycle replacement, helmet, cycling electronics, clothing
Loss of Consortium Impact on spousal or family relationships Inability to participate in family life, loss of companionship

Additional considerations: In cases involving drunk or distracted driving, punitive damages may also be available under Civil Code 3294. Punitive damages are designed to punish particularly reckless conduct and can substantially increase the total recovery. If the accident resulted in a wrongful death, surviving family members may pursue a separate wrongful death claim under CCP 377.60.

How We Help

Bicycle accident cases require specialized knowledge of both California traffic law and the unique dynamics of cyclist-vehicle collisions. Here is how RV Litigation Group PC builds and fights your case:

1. Thorough Accident Investigation

We act quickly to preserve critical evidence before it disappears. Our team obtains police reports, photographs the accident scene, secures surveillance camera footage from nearby businesses, downloads traffic signal data, and works with accident reconstruction experts when necessary. In bicycle cases, physical evidence like skid marks, vehicle damage patterns, and the cyclist's final resting position can be decisive in establishing fault.

2. Establishing Driver Negligence

We identify every traffic law the driver violated — CVC 21760 (three-foot rule), CVC 22517 (unsafe door opening), CVC 23123.5 (cell phone use), CVC 21717 (unsafe merging into bike lane) — and use those violations as evidence of negligence per se. Under California's negligence per se doctrine (Evidence Code 669), a traffic violation that causes injury creates a presumption of negligence, shifting the burden to the driver to prove they were not at fault.

3. Fighting Comparative Fault Arguments

Insurance companies routinely try to blame the cyclist — arguing they were not wearing a helmet, were riding outside the bike lane, or were not visible enough. We counter these tactics aggressively. California does not require adult cyclists to wear helmets, and there are numerous legal exceptions to the bike lane requirement under CVC 21208. We present the evidence, the law, and expert testimony to minimize or eliminate any comparative fault allocation against you.

4. Documenting the Full Scope of Damages

Bicycle accident injuries often require long-term medical treatment. We work with your medical providers to document the full extent of your injuries, obtain life care plans from qualified experts, calculate future medical costs and lost earning capacity, and retain economists who can present these figures in a way that maximizes your recovery. We do not settle until we understand the complete picture of your damages.

5. Aggressive Insurance Negotiation

Insurance companies have teams of adjusters and lawyers whose job is to pay you as little as possible. We handle all communication with the insurance company, reject lowball settlement offers, and negotiate from a position of strength backed by thorough documentation and a willingness to go to trial. If the insurer will not offer fair compensation, we file suit and prepare for litigation.

6. Trial-Ready Representation

Most personal injury cases settle before trial, but the best settlements come when the insurance company knows your attorney is prepared to take the case to a jury. Our attorneys have courtroom experience and are fully prepared to present your case at trial if negotiations do not produce a fair result. We retain accident reconstruction experts, medical professionals, and economists who can testify persuasively on your behalf.
