Pedestrian Accidents in California

California consistently ranks among the most dangerous states in the nation for pedestrians. More than 1,000 pedestrians are killed on California roads each year, and thousands more suffer life-altering injuries. In the Bay Area alone, cities like San Jose and San Francisco see hundreds of pedestrian-involved collisions annually, often at intersections, crosswalks, and in residential neighborhoods throughout Santa Clara County.

Pedestrian accidents produce some of the most catastrophic injuries in personal injury law. When a several-thousand-pound vehicle strikes an unprotected person, the results are devastating: traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, shattered bones, internal organ trauma, and death. If you or a family member was struck by a vehicle in San Jose, San Francisco, Gilroy, Morgan Hill, or anywhere in the Bay Area, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC are here to fight for the compensation you deserve.

What the Law Says

CVC 21950 — Driver's Duty to Yield at Crosswalks

"The driver of a vehicle shall yield the right-of-way to a pedestrian crossing the roadway within any marked crosswalk or within any unmarked crosswalk at an intersection." — California Vehicle Code Section 21950(a)

This is the cornerstone of California pedestrian law. Drivers must yield to pedestrians in both marked and unmarked crosswalks. An unmarked crosswalk exists at every intersection where two roads meet, even if there are no painted lines. CVC 21950(b) also requires that no pedestrian shall suddenly leave a curb and walk or run into the path of a vehicle so close as to constitute an immediate hazard — but this does not relieve a driver of the duty to exercise due care for pedestrian safety.

CVC 21954 — Pedestrians Outside Crosswalks

"Every pedestrian upon a roadway at any point other than within a marked crosswalk or within an unmarked crosswalk at an intersection shall yield the right-of-way to all vehicles upon the roadway so near as to constitute an immediate hazard." — California Vehicle Code Section 21954(a)

When a pedestrian is outside of a crosswalk, the pedestrian must yield to vehicles. However, CVC 21954(b) makes clear that this provision "shall not relieve the driver of a vehicle from the duty to exercise due care for the safety of any pedestrian upon a roadway." In practice, this means a driver who hits a pedestrian outside a crosswalk can still be held liable if the driver was speeding, distracted, or otherwise negligent.

CVC 21955 — Jaywalking

"Between adjacent intersections controlled by traffic control signal devices or by police officers, pedestrians shall not cross the roadway at any place except in a crosswalk." — California Vehicle Code Section 21955

California updated its jaywalking laws in 2023 with the Freedom to Walk Act (AB 2147), which limits when police can cite pedestrians for jaywalking. An officer may only issue a citation when a reasonably careful person would realize there is an immediate danger of a collision. Even when a pedestrian violates CVC 21955, this does not automatically bar them from recovering damages — California's comparative fault rules still apply.

Civil Code 1714 — General Duty of Care

"Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person." — California Civil Code Section 1714(a)

This foundational negligence statute establishes that every person — including every driver — owes a duty of ordinary care to others. When a driver breaches this duty by texting, speeding, running a red light, driving under the influence, or failing to watch for pedestrians, and that breach causes injury, the driver is liable for the resulting damages.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate common pedestrian accident cases our attorneys handle across the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Crosswalk Hit in Downtown San Jose

A pedestrian is crossing Santa Clara Street in a marked crosswalk during the evening commute. A driver making a left turn fails to check the crosswalk and strikes the pedestrian, causing a broken pelvis and traumatic brain injury. The driver violated CVC 21950(a) by failing to yield. The pedestrian has a strong claim for medical expenses, lost income, pain and suffering, and long-term rehabilitation costs.

Example 2 — Distracted Driver in San Francisco

A rideshare driver checking his phone app runs a red light at a busy intersection in the Financial District and strikes a pedestrian in the crosswalk. The pedestrian suffers a spinal cord injury requiring surgery. Both the driver and the rideshare company may be liable. The driver's phone records and the vehicle's dashcam footage become critical evidence in establishing negligence under Civil Code 1714.

Example 3 — Parking Lot Accident

A mother and her child are walking through a grocery store parking lot in Santa Clara County when a driver backing out of a parking space strikes the child. Parking lot accidents are common and often involve drivers who fail to check their mirrors or use backup cameras. The property owner may also share liability if the lot lacked adequate pedestrian walkways, signage, or lighting.

Example 4 — School Zone Incident in Morgan Hill

A speeding driver in a school zone near a Morgan Hill elementary school strikes a teenager crossing the street after dismissal. School zones carry reduced speed limits for a reason. A driver traveling above the posted 25 mph limit in a school zone demonstrates clear negligence. The teenager's injuries — a compound leg fracture and facial lacerations — result in substantial medical bills, months of missed school, and emotional trauma.

What's at Stake

Pedestrian accident victims can pursue multiple categories of damages. The value of your case depends on the severity of your injuries, the impact on your daily life, and the degree of the driver's fault.

Damage Category Type Examples
Medical Expenses Economic Emergency care, surgeries, hospitalization, rehabilitation, future medical needs
Lost Income Economic Missed work during recovery, diminished earning capacity, lost career opportunities
Pain & Suffering Non-Economic Physical pain, emotional distress, PTSD, anxiety, loss of enjoyment of life
Disability & Disfigurement Non-Economic Permanent impairment, scarring, loss of mobility, need for assistive devices
Wrongful Death Economic / Non-Economic Funeral costs, loss of financial support, loss of companionship, survivor grief
Punitive Damages Punitive DUI drivers, hit-and-run, reckless driving — awarded to punish egregious conduct

Comparative fault: California follows a pure comparative negligence system (Li v. Yellow Cab Co., 1975). Even if you were partially at fault — for example, jaywalking or wearing dark clothing at night — your damages are reduced by your percentage of fault but never eliminated. A pedestrian found 30% at fault for a $1,000,000 claim still recovers $700,000.

How We Help

Pedestrian accident cases demand aggressive investigation and skilled negotiation. Insurance companies routinely try to blame the pedestrian, minimize injuries, or push quick lowball settlements. Here is what our team does to protect your claim:

1. Immediate Investigation and Evidence Preservation

Critical evidence disappears quickly after a pedestrian accident. Traffic camera footage is overwritten, skid marks fade, and witnesses forget details. We deploy investigators promptly to photograph the scene, obtain surveillance and dashcam footage, pull traffic signal timing data, secure police reports, and preserve electronic evidence from the driver's phone. In cases involving city or county infrastructure, we issue spoliation letters to prevent the destruction of maintenance records, signal timing logs, and prior accident reports for the location.

2. Identifying All Liable Parties

Pedestrian accidents often involve more than just the driver who hit you. We investigate whether additional parties share responsibility: the vehicle owner (if different from the driver), the driver's employer if the driver was on the job, a rideshare company like Uber or Lyft, the city or county if a dangerous road design or malfunctioning signal contributed to the crash, a property owner if poor lighting or obstructed sightlines played a role, or a vehicle manufacturer if a defective braking system or sensor failure was a factor. More liable parties mean more insurance coverage and a better chance of full recovery.

3. Building a Strong Medical Case

Insurance companies scrutinize medical records to find gaps in treatment, pre-existing conditions, or any basis to argue that your injuries are not as serious as claimed. We work closely with your treating physicians and, when needed, retain independent medical experts to document the full extent of your injuries, project future medical needs, and calculate your lifetime care costs. For traumatic brain injuries and spinal cord injuries, we engage neuropsychologists and life care planners to ensure nothing is overlooked.

4. Accident Reconstruction

In disputed-liability cases, we retain accident reconstruction experts who use physical evidence, vehicle damage patterns, pedestrian biomechanics, and traffic engineering data to recreate exactly how the collision occurred. This scientific analysis is particularly important when a driver claims the pedestrian "came out of nowhere" or when the police report is incomplete or inaccurate. A well-prepared reconstruction can shift liability decisively in your favor.

5. Aggressive Insurance Negotiation

Most pedestrian accident cases settle before trial, but only if the insurance company believes you are prepared to go to court. We present fully documented demand packages with itemized damages, expert opinions, and legal authority. We do not accept lowball offers, and we are transparent with you about the value of your case at every stage. If the insurer refuses to offer fair compensation, we file suit and proceed toward trial without hesitation.

6. Government Claims and Short Deadlines

If a government vehicle or dangerous public infrastructure contributed to your accident — a city bus, a poorly designed intersection, a missing crosswalk signal, a broken streetlight — you must file a government tort claim within six months of the incident under the California Government Claims Act (Gov. Code 900 et seq.). Missing this deadline can permanently bar your claim. We handle these accelerated timelines and navigate the additional procedural requirements that government entity claims demand.
