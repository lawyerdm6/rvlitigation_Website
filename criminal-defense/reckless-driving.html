What Is Reckless Driving?

Reckless driving under California Vehicle Code 23103 is the act of operating a vehicle with willful or wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property. It is a broad charge that covers a range of dangerous driving behaviors — from excessive speeding and aggressive lane changes to street racing and ignoring traffic signals. Reckless driving is also significant as a common plea-bargain outcome in DUI cases, known as a "wet reckless" under VC 23103.5.

If you have been charged with reckless driving in San Jose, San Francisco, Fremont, Mountain View, or anywhere in Santa Clara County, the attorneys at RV Litigation Group PC can help. Whether you are facing a standalone reckless driving charge or negotiating a wet reckless as an alternative to DUI, our defense team will work to minimize the impact on your record and your life.

What the Law Says

Vehicle Code 23103(a) — Reckless Driving on Highway

"A person who drives a vehicle upon a highway in willful or wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property is guilty of reckless driving." — California Vehicle Code Section 23103(a)

The statute requires "willful or wanton disregard" — meaning you intentionally drove in a dangerous manner or were aware that your driving created an unreasonable risk and chose to continue anyway. Mere negligence or a simple traffic violation is not enough. The prosecution must prove that your driving rose to the level of a conscious disregard for safety.

Vehicle Code 23103(b) — Reckless Driving in Parking Facility

"A person who drives a vehicle in an off-street parking facility... in willful or wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property is guilty of reckless driving." — California Vehicle Code Section 23103(b)

This subsection extends reckless driving to off-street parking facilities — including parking lots, parking garages, and private parking structures. Doing donuts in a parking lot, racing through a parking garage, or driving recklessly through a crowded shopping center parking lot can all result in charges under this subsection.

Vehicle Code 23103.5 — Wet Reckless (DUI Plea Bargain)

"If the prosecution agrees to a plea of guilty or nolo contendere to a charge of a violation of Section 23103 in satisfaction of, or as a substitute for, an original charge of a violation of Section 23152, the prosecution shall state for the record a factual basis for the satisfaction or substitution, including whether or not there had been consumption of an alcoholic beverage..." — California Vehicle Code Section 23103.5

A "wet reckless" is a plea bargain — not an arrestable offense. Prosecutors may offer it when the DUI case has weaknesses, such as a borderline BAC, procedural errors, or lack of impairment evidence. The advantages over a DUI conviction include shorter probation, no mandatory license suspension (though the DMV may still take action), shorter DUI school, lower fines, and no mandatory jail time. However, it still counts as a prior DUI offense for 10 years.

Real-World Examples

These scenarios illustrate how reckless driving charges commonly arise in the Bay Area:

Example 1 — Excessive Speed on Highway

A driver on Highway 101 near San Jose weaves through traffic at over 100 mph, cutting off multiple vehicles. CHP officers pull him over after a brief pursuit. His speed and lane-changing pattern demonstrate willful disregard for safety, supporting a reckless driving (VC 23103) charge rather than just a speeding ticket.

Example 2 — Wet Reckless Plea Bargain

A woman in San Francisco is arrested for DUI with a BAC of .09% — just barely over the legal limit. Her attorney negotiates a wet reckless (VC 23103.5) plea, which results in shorter probation, a shorter alcohol program, and no mandatory license suspension from the court — significantly better than a standard DUI conviction.

Example 3 — Street Racing

Two drivers race each other on a public road in Milpitas at night, reaching speeds of 80 mph in a 35 mph zone. Both are pulled over and charged with reckless driving and potentially speed contest (VC 23109). Street racing is one of the clearest examples of willful disregard for safety.

Example 4 — Emergency Circumstances

A father in Gilroy drives aggressively to the hospital because his child is having a severe allergic reaction. He runs two red lights and exceeds the speed limit by 30 mph. While his driving was objectively dangerous, the emergency circumstances may negate the "willful disregard" element — he was not being reckless for reckless' sake but responding to a genuine emergency.

Penalties

Reckless driving is a misdemeanor in California. The penalties depend on whether injury occurred and whether the charge is a standalone offense or a wet reckless plea bargain.

Additional consequences: A reckless driving conviction adds 2 points to your DMV record, which can lead to a negligent operator suspension if combined with other violations. Your insurance rates will increase significantly. A wet reckless counts as a prior DUI for 10 years, meaning a subsequent DUI will be treated as a second offense with enhanced penalties. For commercial drivers, a reckless driving conviction can result in CDL suspension or revocation.

Legal Defenses

Reckless driving charges can be challenged on several grounds. The prosecution must prove a specific mental state — willful disregard — which is a high bar:

1. Driving Was Not Willful or Wanton

The prosecution must prove more than careless driving — they must show you acted with conscious disregard for safety. If your driving was merely negligent (such as briefly exceeding the speed limit or making a wide turn), it does not rise to the level of reckless driving. We argue that your conduct, while potentially unsafe, did not demonstrate the willful intent required by the statute.

2. Emergency Circumstances

If you were driving aggressively because of a genuine emergency — rushing someone to the hospital, fleeing a dangerous situation, or responding to an urgent family matter — the "willful disregard" element may not be satisfied. We present evidence of the emergency that motivated your driving behavior.

3. No Endangerment

If no other people or property were actually at risk — for example, speeding on an empty road at 3:00 AM — the element of disregard for the safety of "persons or property" may be difficult for the prosecution to prove. We examine the specific circumstances of the driving and challenge whether anyone was actually endangered.

4. Misidentification

In cases involving officer observation from a distance, helicopter pursuit, or reports from other drivers, the wrong person can be identified as the reckless driver. We challenge the identification evidence, examine whether the vehicle was properly identified, and present alibi evidence when available.

5. Negotiate for Reduction

Even when the evidence supports the charge, an experienced attorney can often negotiate a reduction to a lesser offense — such as an infraction for unsafe speed (VC 22350) or a basic moving violation. These carry fewer points, lower fines, no jail time, and less impact on your insurance and driving record. We advocate for the most favorable resolution possible.
